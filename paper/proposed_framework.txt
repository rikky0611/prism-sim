\section{Proposed Framework}
\label{sec:framework}

\subsection{Overview and Design Goals}
\label{sec:framework-overview}

Section~\ref{sec:rw} identified a gap between two bodies of work: deployed procedural assistants instantiate mixed initiative through one or two local triggers, while decision-theoretic and learning-based formulations of assistance supply a sequential, cost-aware language but leave domain-level modeling open.
We close this gap for communication-heavy procedural assistance by formulating mixed initiative as a cooperative two-agent POMDP in which the human and the assistant are both decision-makers and share a single objective.
Rather than designing the assistant's policy directly, we specify a small number of communicative actions, a memory-modulated failure model, and a sensing model, and let policies emerge from optimization.

The framework is designed to expose how three structural factors shape the emergent mixed-initiative behavior:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Communication regime}: the asymmetric per-action costs of
        narrating, asking a question, reminding, and confirming.
  \item \textbf{Expected failure cost}: the product of memory-modulated
        failure probability and per-step failure cost, which determines the
        stakes of any given step.
  \item \textbf{Sensing fidelity}: the assistant's observation-noise
        dynamics, including how much narration reduces noise and how durable
        that reduction is.
\end{enumerate}

Holding the environment dynamics and the action vocabulary fixed and sweeping these three axes lets us isolate their causal effect on the learned policy and on the resulting \emph{division of communicative labor} between user and assistant.

\subsection{Two-Agent Cooperative POMDP}
\label{sec:framework-pomdp}

We model the problem as a cooperative, decentralized POMDP (Dec-POMDP / two-agent POSG with shared reward) \(\mathcal{M} = \langle \mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}^{h}, \mathcal{A}^{a}, T, \Omega^{h}, \Omega^{a}, O^{h}, O^{a}, r, \gamma \rangle\), where superscript \(h\) indexes the \emph{human} agent and \(a\) the \emph{assistant} agent.
At every tick, both agents act simultaneously and receive the same scalar reward.

\paragraph{Agents.}
The \emph{human} is the agent performing the procedural task.
The human has perfect knowledge of which step it is currently performing, but its memory of the next critical step decays over time, which raises the risk of a failure if the step is reached without sufficient memory.
The human can stay silent, narrate the current step, or ask a clarifying question about the nearest upcoming critical step.

The \emph{assistant} is an external agent (e.g.\ a wearable agent or ambient AI) that does not perform the task and does not directly observe the human's memory or the true step.
It maintains a belief over the human's procedural state from a noisy identity sensor and the human's communicative actions, and chooses among staying silent, confirming the human's current step, or sending a reminder about a specific upcoming critical step.

\subsection{State and Dynamics}
\label{sec:framework-state}

A procedural task is a sequence of \(N\) steps with a designated subset \(\mathcal{C} \subseteq \{0, \dots, N-1\}\) of critical steps; only critical steps can fail.
The latent state at tick \(t\) is \[ x_t = (s_t,\; \tau_t,\; m_t,\; n_t), \] where \(s_t \in \{0, \dots, N-1, \bot\}\) is the current step (\(\bot\) denotes task completion), \(\tau_t \in \mathbb{N}\) is the elapsed time within the current step, \(m_t \in [0, m_{\max}]^{N}\) is a per-step memory vector, and \(n_t \in [n_{\min}, n_{\mathrm{base}}]\) is the assistant's observation-noise level.
We treat the procedural process as semi-Markov: each step \(s\) has a stochastic dwell time with mean \(\mu_s\) and standard deviation \(\sigma_s\), and step transitions follow an empirical ordering distribution sampled at episode start.

\paragraph{Memory dynamics.}
Memory updates additively from communicative actions, then decays in batch at step transitions: \[ m_{t+1}[i] \;=\; m_t[i] \;+\; \delta_{q}\,\mathbb{1}[a^{h}_t \!=\! \texttt{question}_{\mathrm{next}} \wedge i = \nu(s_t) \wedge i \notin Q_t] \;+\; \delta_{r}\,\mathbb{1}[a^{a}_t \!=\! \texttt{remind}_i], \] clipped to \([m_{\infty}, m_{\max}]\), where \(\nu(s_t)\) is the human's nearest upcoming critical step from \(s_t\) and \(Q_t \subseteq \mathcal{C}\) is the set of critical steps the human has already asked about this episode (ask-once).
At each step transition we apply a single batched decay \[ m \;\leftarrow\; \max\!\bigl(m \cdot (1 - \lambda_{\mathrm{forget}})^{\tau+1},\; m_{\infty}\bigr), \] which keeps memory stable \emph{within} a step (so that the human can rely on the same memory level while performing it) and concentrates forgetting at step boundaries.
The floor \(m_{\infty}\) is the \emph{baseline retention level} for procedurally well-learned material: empirically, long-term retention of over-trained content plateaus rather than decaying to zero (Bahrick's permastore phenomenon~\cite{bahrick1984permastore}).
The exponential decay form between boundaries is a discrete-time approximation; we adopt it for tractability while noting that retention is better fit globally by a power law over very long lags~\cite{wixted2007wickelgren}.

\paragraph{Failure model.}
Conditional on completing critical step \(i\), failure occurs with probability \[ f_i(m) \;=\; f_{0}\,\bigl(1 - \sigma\bigl(\beta\,(m[i] - \theta)\bigr)\bigr), \] where \(\sigma\) is the logistic function, \(\theta\) is the activation level for 50\% retrieval, and \(\beta\) controls transition sharpness.
This is the prospective-memory retrieval probability of Einstein and McDaniel's multiprocess framework~\cite{mcdaniel2000multiprocess,einstein2005prospective}: a critical step fails iff retrieval of the associated procedural action fails, with the assistant's reminder acting as an event-based focal cue.
Failure incurs a per-step cost \(c_{\mathrm{fail}}(i)\) determined by the step's criticality.
Non-critical steps cannot fail.
The expected failure cost of step \(i\) is therefore \(\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{fail}_i] = f_i(m) \cdot c_{\mathrm{fail}}(i)\): it depends \emph{both} on memory (controllable by communication) and on intrinsic stakes (a property of the step).

\paragraph{Communication cost.}
Per-action costs (\(c_{\mathrm{nar}}, c_{q}, c_{\mathrm{remind}}, c_{\mathrm{confirm}}\)) carry a base attention demand, modulated by three context-dependent terms that route the \emph{appropriateness} of communication through the cost structure rather than leaving it implicit.
First, a \emph{mid-subtask penalty}: following the empirical finding that interrupting at coarse task boundaries cuts resumption time and error rates by roughly a factor of two compared to mid-subtask interruption~\cite{adamczyk2004ifnot,iqbal2007disruption}, an assistant action issued at elapsed time \(\tau\) costs \(c^{a}_{\mathrm{base}} + c_{\mathrm{mid}}\,\mathbb{1}[\tau > \tau_{\mathrm{bound}}]\), where \(\tau_{\mathrm{bound}}\) is a protected boundary window of a few ticks immediately after each step transition.
Second, a \emph{graded off-timing penalty} for reminders: a reminder about a step at distance \(d = |i - s_t|\) from the human's current step incurs \(c_{\mathrm{off}} \cdot \max(0, d-1)\), so reminders about the current or immediately upcoming step are penalty-free while those further out grow linearly more costly --- a prospective-memory cue delivered far from its trigger is both less useful and more disruptive.
Third, a \emph{confirmation-habituation} term: the \(k\)-th confirmation within an episode costs \(c_{\mathrm{confirm}}\,(1+\gamma)^{k-1}\), reflecting alert/alarm fatigue --- repeated generic check-ins lose value and become irritating~\cite{cvach2012alarm}.
Together these give each communicative channel a literature-grounded ``naturalness'' constraint, so that human-like timing emerges from optimization rather than being hand-specified.

\paragraph{Sensing dynamics.}
The assistant's identity sensor is corrupted by Gaussian noise gated by \(n_t\): with probability \(n_t\) the observation is \(\mathrm{round}(s_t + \mathcal{N}(0,1))\), otherwise it equals \(s_t\).
Narration by the human resets \(n_t\) to a noise floor \(n_{\min}\); on all other ticks \(n_t\) recovers exponentially toward the baseline \(n_{\mathrm{base}}\): \[ n_{t+1} = n_{\mathrm{base}} - (n_{\mathrm{base}} - n_{\min})\, \exp(-\lambda_{n}\, \Delta t_{\mathrm{nar}}), \] where \(\Delta t_{\mathrm{nar}}\) is the number of ticks since the last narration.
The pair \((n_{\min},\, \lambda_n)\) controls how \emph{strong} and how \emph{durable} a single narration is.

\subsection{Action Spaces}
\label{sec:framework-actions}

The two agents share three action categories --- silent, broad utterance, and step-targeted utterance --- but with an intentional asymmetry in how the targeted action is parametrized:
\[ \mathcal{A}^{h} = \{\texttt{silent},\, \texttt{narrate},\, \texttt{question}_{\mathrm{next}}\}, \qquad \mathcal{A}^{a} = \{\texttt{silent},\, \texttt{confirm}\} \cup \{\texttt{remind}_i : i \in \mathcal{C}\}. \]
The human's \texttt{question}\(_{\mathrm{next}}\) is a single action that dynamically targets the nearest upcoming critical step \(\arg\min_{i \in \mathcal{C}}\{i \mid i \geq s_t\}\) (if any); the assistant's \texttt{remind}\(_i\) is explicitly parametrized by step \(i\).
This reflects an asymmetry in how each agent forms a referring expression to a procedural step: a human asking "what's next?" implicitly points to whichever critical step is coming up, while an assistant that has been tracking task structure can elect to remind about any specific step.
The effects form a clean two-by-two over \{who initiates\} \(\times\) \{what is updated\}.
The non-targeted utterances both sharpen the assistant's tracking: \texttt{narrate} resets the assistant's observation noise but costs the \emph{human} attention, while \texttt{confirm} achieves the same noise reset --- the assistant solicits a confirmation of the current step and the human's response reveals identity --- but costs the \emph{assistant} a delivery action.
The targeted utterances both boost the human's memory at a critical step: \texttt{question}\(_{\mathrm{next}}\) raises \(m\) at the resolved upcoming step, \texttt{remind}\(_i\) raises \(m[i]\) at the explicitly chosen step.
A ``human-likeness'' ask-once constraint applies: each critical step's question can only boost memory the first time the human asks about it in an episode (people do not re-ask a question they have already resolved), so the policy must time its single question per step well rather than poll repeatedly.
The asymmetry of \emph{who pays which cost} for an effect that is otherwise identical is what makes the cost structure non-trivial.

\subsection{Observations}
\label{sec:framework-obs}

\paragraph{Human observation.}
The human observes its own current step identity, elapsed time \(\tau_t\), its own memory at the current step \(m_t[s_t]\), the assistant's last action, the current noise state \(n_t\), and a per-critical-step \emph{asked} indicator \(\mathbf{q}_t \in \{0,1\}^{|\mathcal{C}|}\) recording which critical steps it has already asked about this episode.
The asked indicator makes the ask-once constraint observable: without it, a repeated \texttt{question}\(_i\) would appear to the policy as a randomly-effective action governed by hidden state, and the human's decision problem would not be Markov in its observation.
The human therefore has perfect self-knowledge but receives the assistant's communicative actions through the same channel as the assistant uses to act.

\paragraph{Assistant observation.}
The assistant does not observe \(s_t\), \(\tau_t\), or \(m_t\) directly.
It maintains a joint semi-Markov belief \(b_t(s, \tau)\) over step identity and elapsed time, updated each tick by (i) prior propagation under the dwell distributions and the empirical identity transition matrix, (ii) a Bayesian update from the noisy identity observation, and (iii) a hard reset to the row \(b_t(s_t^*, \cdot)\) when the human narrates and reveals \(s_t^* = s_t\).
In addition, the assistant maintains an estimate \(\hat{m}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{C}|}\) of memory for critical steps that is built only from observed interactions (its own reminders and observed questions, with batched decay), with no access to ground-truth memory.
The full assistant observation is the marginal step belief \(\sum_\tau b_t(\cdot, \tau)\), the expected dwell \(\mathbb{E}[\tau \mid s]\), the joint belief \(b_t\), the memory estimate \(\hat{m}_t\), the human's last action, and the same per-critical-step asked indicator \(\mathbf{q}_t\) so that the assistant can tell when the human's questions are spent.
This setup follows the semi-Markov POMDP formulation used in PrISM-style trackers~\cite{DBLP:journals/imwut/ArakawaYMNRDRMC22}, but here the belief is consumed by an RL policy rather than a hand-coded controller.

\subsection{Reward and the Three Design Axes}
\label{sec:framework-reward}

The shared per-tick reward decomposes additively into a communication term, a failure term, and a terminal completion bonus:

\begin{align*}
r_t = \;
& -\, c(a^{h}_t)
\;-\; c(a^{a}_t)
\;-\; c_{\mathrm{off}}\, \max(0, d_t - 1) \\
& -\, \mathbb{1}[\text{step completed at }t \text{ and failed}]
       \cdot c_{\mathrm{fail}}(s_t) \\
& +\, \mathbb{1}[s_{t+1} = \bot] \cdot R_{\mathrm{complete}},
\end{align*}

where \(c(a^{h}_t)\) and \(c(a^{a}_t)\) are the context-modulated per-action costs defined above (base cost plus mid-subtask and habituation terms), and \(d_t = |i - s_t|\) is the step-distance of a reminder about step \(i\) (zero for non-reminder actions).
The graded off-timing penalty \(c_{\mathrm{off}}\,\max(0, d_t-1)\) discourages reminders that are technically informative but interrupt about a step far from the human's current focus.
\(R_{\mathrm{complete}}\) provides a clear terminal signal so that policies do not converge to arbitrarily delaying the task.

This reward structure exposes three orthogonal \emph{design axes} that together define a regime:

\paragraph{(1) Communication regime.}
The vector \(\mathbf{c}_{\mathrm{comm}} = (c_{\mathrm{nar}}, c_{q}, c_{\mathrm{remind}}, c_{\mathrm{confirm}}, c_{\mathrm{off}})\) specifies the asymmetric cost of each communicative action, separately for the human and the assistant.
For example, a regime with cheap narration and expensive reminders favors solutions in which the human discloses state and the assistant remains nearly silent; a regime with cheap reminders and expensive questions favors a more proactive assistant.
This axis directly controls the \emph{division of communicative labor}.

\paragraph{(2) Expected failure cost.}
A step's stakes are governed by the product of two terms: the memory-modulated failure probability \(f_i(m)\), itself shaped by \((f_0, k, \lambda_{\mathrm{forget}}, \delta_q, \delta_r, m_{\mathrm{init}})\), and the per-step failure cost \(c_{\mathrm{fail}}(i)\), shaped by step criticality.
Increasing either factor raises the marginal value of any communicative action that boosts memory.
Sweeping this axis lets us study regimes from low-stakes (failure cost dominated by communication cost) to high-stakes (a single failure dominates the episode return).

\paragraph{(3) Sensing fidelity.}
The triple \((n_{\mathrm{base}}, n_{\min}, \lambda_n)\) controls how informative the assistant's identity sensor is in the absence of narration, how much a single narration reduces noise, and how quickly that reduction decays.
Sensing fidelity changes the value of human-initiated state disclosure: when narration is durable (\(\lambda_n\) small), occasional narration suffices for the assistant to track the human; when narration fades quickly, the assistant must rely more on its prior or actively seek confirmation.

These three axes correspond to the three factors that recurred in Section~\ref{sec:rw} but were rarely combined within a single deployed system: communication cost, intervention stakes, and observability.
Holding everything else fixed and sweeping each axis independently is the core experimental device of this paper.

\subsection{Policy Learning}
\label{sec:framework-learning}

We learn the joint policy with Independent PPO (IPPO) using alternating best-response training: in each round we hold one agent's policy fixed and train the other against it for a fixed number of environment steps, then swap roles.
Both policies are optimized against the same shared reward, so each best-response step improves a lower bound on the cooperative return.
Training across regimes shares all hyperparameters; only the regime parameters \((\mathbf{c}_{\mathrm{comm}}, c_{\mathrm{fail}}, \lambda_{\mathrm{forget}}, n_{\mathrm{base}}, n_{\min}, \lambda_n)\) change.
This design lets us attribute differences in the learned mixed-initiative behavior to the regime axes rather than to optimizer-specific choices.
