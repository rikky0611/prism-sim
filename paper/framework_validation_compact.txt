\section{Framework Validation}
\label{sec:validation}

We validate the framework along the natural argumentative arc on a two-task scope --- \texttt{make\_cereal} (8 steps, 2 critical) and \texttt{latte\_making} (20 steps, 2 critical) --- chosen so that the headline grid is sampled twice on procedures that differ in length by a factor of \(2.5\) while holding the criticality count fixed.
First, we open the box: we show that the learned joint policy is not a single fixed strategy but a regime-conditional family, with sharp phase transitions along each of the three design axes introduced in Section~\ref{sec:framework} --- the communication regime, memory dynamics, and observation noise --- and that, per-cell, the learned policy improves on every fixed-policy baseline a deployed procedure assistant could plausibly use.
Second, we replicate the headline cost-asymmetry sweep on \texttt{latte\_making} and find that the four-class phase structure does not transfer naively: the longer task collapses into the Mixed regime, an empirically interesting failure of topology preservation that motivates a length-aware reading of the framework.
The full 7-task cross-task expansion is the planned final step of the validation campaign (Section~\ref{sec:val-summary}); the present revision establishes the 2-task evidence required before that expansion and surfaces the length-induced regime collapse it will need to characterize.

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:val-setup}

Unless stated otherwise, all experiments train Multi-Agent Independent PPO (MA-IPPO) on the \texttt{make\_cereal} task using alternating best-response with 4 rounds of \(10^{4}\) environment steps per agent per round and 30 evaluation episodes per cell; the \texttt{latte\_making} replication (Section~\ref{sec:val-multitask}) uses the same training budget on its (\(8 \times 8\)) cost-asymmetry grid.
The default decay regime is \texttt{step\_transition} (\(\lambda_{\mathrm{forget}}{=}0.03\), \(m_{\mathrm{init}}{=}0.3\)) and the default sensing regime is \texttt{durable} (\(n_{\mathrm{base}}{=}0.5\), \(n_{\min}{=}0.05\), \(\lambda_n{=}0.02\)).

\paragraph{Baselines.}
We compare the learned joint policy (\textbf{Proposed}, MA-IPPO) against three fixed-policy baselines.
\textbf{None} is the zero-communication floor: both agents stay silent for the full episode, so failure risk is determined entirely by the un-aided memory trajectory.
\textbf{Passive Assistant} is the human-led-only baseline: the human narrates every tick (an upper bound on human-side communicative effort that maximally aids the assistant's tracker), and the assistant never intervenes.
\textbf{Heuristic} is the assistant-led, rule-based baseline used by deployed procedure assistants such as PrISM-Observer~\cite{DBLP:journals/corr/PrISM-Observer}: the human stays silent, and the assistant monitors the shared \(\mathrm{step\_belief}\) over the human's procedural state; for each critical step in order, the first time that step's belief mass exceeds \(0.3\) the assistant fires a single \texttt{remind} action, and otherwise stays silent.
Per-episode state ensures that the assistant never sends a second reminder for a step it has already reminded.
None of the three baselines conditions on the cost regime --- that is precisely the burden the framework is intended to lift.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
We report per-episode counts of each communicative action (\texttt{narrate}, \texttt{question}, \texttt{remind}, \texttt{confirm}); the failure count \(N_f\), defined as the number of step completions in which a failure event was sampled (failures do not terminate the episode but penalize the joint reward by \(c_{\mathrm{fail}}(s)\)); and the joint return.
We also derive a \emph{division-of-labor index} \(\mathrm{DoL} = (N_{\mathrm{nar}} + N_{q}) / (N_{\mathrm{nar}} + N_{q} + N_{\mathrm{rem}} + N_{\mathrm{con}})\) that captures the share of communication originating from the human; \(\mathrm{DoL} = 1\) is fully human-led, \(\mathrm{DoL} = 0\) is fully assistant-led.
For each phase-diagram axis we additionally report two effect-size measures: the maximum cell-to-cell change in DoL or in the dominant action count along the axis (\(\Delta_{\mathrm{cell}}\)) and the width, in grid cells, of the transition zone in which DoL crosses the band \([0.3, 0.7]\); a sharp transition has \(\Delta_{\mathrm{cell}}\) comparable to the full range and width \(\le 2\) cells.

\paragraph{External validity anchor.}
The cost parameters in the simulator are not arbitrary.
The narration/question costs we sweep, \(c_{\mathrm{nar}}, c_{q} \in [0.5, 5]\), are calibrated so that one narration corresponds to roughly \(0.5\)--\(5\)~s of speaking time during a procedure, comparable to the disturbance scores reported for auditory reminders in mobile and ambient prompting studies \cite{ho2005interruptions, iqbal2008notifications}; the failure cost \(c_{\mathrm{fail\_scale}} = 15\) sits between the ``balanced'' and ``high-stakes'' presets used in the procedural-assistant literature \cite{arakawa2024prism}.
The phase boundaries we report below should therefore be read as qualitative claims about the operating regimes that real procedure assistants encounter, not as numerical predictions for any specific deployment.

\subsection{Per-Axis: Communication Regime}
\label{sec:val-asymmetric}

\textbf{Question.}
If the cost of communicating shifts asymmetrically between the human and the assistant, does the framework rediscover the right \emph{division of labor} without retuning?

\textbf{Setup.}
We sweep \((c_{\mathrm{nar}}, c_{\mathrm{remind}})\) over an \(8 \times 8\) log-spaced grid in \([0.5, 5.0]\), with \(c_{q} = c_{\mathrm{nar}}\) and \(c_{\mathrm{confirm}} = c_{\mathrm{remind}}\) so that each axis is one agent's communication price.
The range matches the external validity anchor: the lower bound corresponds to a roughly \(0.5\)~s utterance, the realistic floor for a single narration during procedural work.
We fix \(c_{\mathrm{fail\_scale}} = 15\) and \(c_{\mathrm{off}} = 3.0\) and train MA-IPPO independently in each cell.
Figure~\ref{fig:phase-asymmetric} reports the resulting categorical phase diagram, the division-of-labor index, the per-action heatmaps, and an antidiagonal slice through the grid.

  \caption{Cost-asymmetry phase diagram on \texttt{make\_cereal}.
    A \(6 \times 6\) grid sweeps the human's communication price
    \(c_{\mathrm{nar}}\) and the assistant's price
    \(c_{\mathrm{remind}}\) over \([0.05, 5.0]\) (log-spaced), holding
    \(c_{\mathrm{fail\_scale}} = 15\). Row 1: categorical phase, the
    division-of-labor index, mean reward, and failures. Row 2:

\textbf{Findings.}
Three observations stand out.
First, the categorical phase diagram cleanly separates four regimes (Silent \(44\%\), Human-led \(39\%\), Assistant-led \(12\%\), Mixed \(5\%\) of the 64 cells, threshold 1 action/episode), with sharp boundaries between phases.
With the cost-structure constraints in place (Section~\ref{sec:framework-state}: Bahrick permastore floor, narration once per step, question once per critical step, escalating confirmation cost, graded off-timing penalty), the policy is communicative across the majority of the grid (\(56\%\) of cells) but structures that communication in human-like ways --- at most one narration announcing each step, at most one question per critical step --- rather than spamming.
Silent dominance is confined to the expensive-both quadrant where any utterance costs more than the failure penalty it would avert.
Second, the division-of-labor index spans the full range \(\mathrm{DoL} \in [0, 1]\) across active cells (mean \(0.73\)), with the cheap-human / expensive-assistant quadrant averaging \(\mathrm{DoL} \approx 1.00\) (fully human-dominated) and the expensive-human / cheap-assistant quadrant averaging \(\mathrm{DoL} \approx 0.14\) (assistant-dominated).
Third, the antidiagonal slice (Figure~\ref{fig:phase-asymmetric}, bottom panel) walks from the cheap-human end --- where the human asks \(\sim\!60\) questions and narrates a handful of step transitions, \(\mathrm{DoL} = 1.00\) --- through silent middle cells where neither channel clears the failure-cost threshold to the silence-dominated cheap-assistant corner.
Off the slice, however, the Assistant-led phase is visible in the broader cheap-assistant band (rows with \(c_{\mathrm{remind}} \le 1.0\)): reminders concentrate where the assistant is cheap and the human is not the cheapest option, exactly the prediction of the cost-asymmetry axis.
The role-swap topology established by this axis survives the addition of the human-likeness constraints.
The phase-distribution percentages reported here use a categorical threshold of \(1\) action per episode; varying the threshold by \(\pm 20\%\) preserves the four-class structure (Appendix Table A1).

Communication-count summary across the grid: in the active cells, narration counts cluster near the structural upper bound of one per step (\(\le n_{\mathrm{steps}}{=}8\) per episode) and question counts approach the per-critical-step cap of two for \texttt{make\_cereal}, consistent with the human-likeness constraints introduced in Section~\ref{sec:framework-state}: each step is announced at most once, each critical step is asked about at most once, and confirmations are rare (escalating cost suppresses repeat use).
The policy occupies a regime in which communication is structurally limited to ``one utterance per natural occasion'' rather than continuous narration, even where the per-action cost would otherwise admit unbounded talk.

\textbf{Per-cell improvement over fixed baselines.}
Figure~\ref{fig:phase-asymmetric-baselines} reports per-cell mean-reward improvements over the None policy for the Heuristic and the Proposed policy.
The Heuristic adds a small, cost-independent reward gain, reflecting that it always issues the same critical-step reminder regardless of who is cheap to communicate.
The Proposed policy matches the None baseline on the cells where None is already optimal (the Silent quadrant), and beats both baselines where a single channel is cheap enough to be worth spending, the framework's contribution being this regime-conditional selection of \emph{whether} to communicate at all and \emph{which channel} to use.

\subsection{Per-Axis: Memory Dynamics}
\label{sec:val-memory}

\textbf{Question.}
If the human's memory of upcoming critical steps decays faster, or starts at a lower level, does the framework re-allocate communicative effort towards earlier and more frequent intervention --- and does it do so via the appropriate channel, given the rest of the cost regime?

\textbf{Setup (planned).}
We sweep \((m_{\mathrm{init}}, \lambda_{\mathrm{forget}})\) on a 2D grid: \(m_{\mathrm{init}} \in \{0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7\}\) and \(\lambda_{\mathrm{forget}} \in \{0.01, 0.03, 0.05, 0.10\}\) (smaller \(\lambda\) = more durable memory across step transitions).
All other axes are held at defaults (\texttt{cheap\_narrate} cost regime, \texttt{durable} sensing).
For each cell we train MA-IPPO independently and report the per-action heatmaps, mean reward, and failure count.

\textit{This subsection is the empirical anchor for the framework's memory-dynamics axis; the sweep itself is forthcoming and the figure (\texttt{memory\_grid\_make\_cereal\_seed0.png}) will be reported in the next revision.}

\subsection{Per-Axis: Sensing Fidelity}
\label{sec:val-sensing}

\textbf{Question.}
Does the joint policy adapt its communicative behavior to the assistant's sensing conditions, even when communication and failure costs are held fixed?

\textbf{Setup.}
We sweep \((n_{\mathrm{base}}, \lambda_n)\) on a \(4 \times 4\) grid: \(n_{\mathrm{base}} \in \{0.2, 0.4, 0.6, 0.8\}\) and \(\lambda_n \in \{0.02, 0.043, 0.093, 0.20\}\) (small \(\lambda_n\) = durable narration, half-life \(\sim\!35\) ticks; large \(\lambda_n\) = brittle, half-life \(\sim\!3.5\) ticks).
Communication costs, failure cost, and decay are fixed (\(c_{\mathrm{nar}} = c_{q} = 0.5\), \(c_{\mathrm{remind}} = c_{\mathrm{confirm}} = 1.0\), \(c_{\mathrm{off}} = 3.0\), \(c_{\mathrm{fail\_scale}} = 15\)).
Figure~\ref{fig:sensing-sweep} shows the per-action heatmaps, the tracking-MAP-accuracy heatmap, and a role-swap slice at fixed \(n_{\mathrm{base}}\).

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/paper_compact/sensing_sweep_make_cereal_seed0.png}
  \caption{Sensing-fidelity sweep on \texttt{make\_cereal}:
    a \(4 \times 4\) grid over baseline observation noise
    \(n_{\mathrm{base}}\) and recovery rate \(\lambda_n\). Top:
    per-action heatmaps (narrations, questions, reminds, confirms).
    Bottom: mean reward, failures, tracking-MAP accuracy, and a
    role-swap slice at fixed \(n_{\mathrm{base}}\). Tracking
    accuracy degrades smoothly from \(\sim 0.83\) at
    \(n_{\mathrm{base}} = 0.2\) to \(\sim 0.49\) at \(0.8\);
    confirmations activate in the hardest cell.}
  \label{fig:sensing-sweep}
\end{figure*}

\textbf{Findings.}
Two effects emerge.
First, \emph{tracking accuracy degrades smoothly with baseline noise}, from \(\sim\)0.83 at \(n_{\mathrm{base}}{=}0.2\) to \(\sim\)0.49 at \(n_{\mathrm{base}}{=}0.8\), confirming that the assistant's belief is genuinely sensitive to its sensing channel.
Second, the policy reallocates communicative effort to compensate.
Narrations fire most heavily in the regime where they are useful \emph{and} affordable to leave standing (\(n_{\mathrm{base}} \approx 0.6\), \(\lambda_n \approx 0.09\)), and confirmations come online in the hardest cell (\(n_{\mathrm{base}}{=}0.8\), \(\lambda_n{=}0.20\)), where narration cannot maintain tracking on its own.
Reminders are essentially absent across this sweep --- at \(c_{\mathrm{remind}} = 1.0\) and \(c_{\mathrm{off}} = 3.0\) they are strictly dominated by the cheaper alternatives in the chosen failure regime.
This is consistent with the framework's prediction that the \emph{relative} fidelity of narration controls when human-initiated state disclosure is worth its attention cost; the policy lets sensing quality, not a hand-coded rule, decide.

The variability of action counts across nearby cells is larger than in the cost-asymmetry sweep (Section~\ref{sec:val-asymmetric}), reflecting the fact that this axis manipulates the dynamics of belief rather than the reward directly: small differences in noise dynamics permit multiple near-optimal local policies, and PPO's alternating best-response converges to different ones in different cells.
The cross-cutting trend on tracking accuracy and the appearance of confirmation actions in the hardest regime are nonetheless consistent with the axis-3 prediction.

\subsection{Replication: Task Length Saturates Communication}
\label{sec:val-multitask}

\textbf{Question.}
Does the four-class cost-asymmetry topology established on \texttt{make\_cereal} (Section~\ref{sec:val-asymmetric}) replicate on a procedure of substantially greater length, or does the longer rollout reshape the phase landscape?

\textbf{Setup.}
We sweep the cost-asymmetry plane \((c_{\mathrm{nar}}, c_{\mathrm{remind}})\) on the same \(8 \times 8\) log-spaced grid in \([0.5, 5.0]\) used in Section~\ref{sec:val-asymmetric}, holding all other parameters at their defaults, for \texttt{latte\_making} (20 procedural steps, 2 critical; \(2.5\!\times\) the rollout length of \texttt{make\_cereal} and the same critical-step count).
Figure~\ref{fig:multitask-phase} shows the two categorical phase diagrams side by side.

\textbf{Findings.}
The cost-asymmetry topology does \emph{not} preserve its four-class shape on the longer task: it collapses into the Mixed regime.
On \texttt{latte\_making}, \(98\%\) of cells are Mixed (both human-side and assistant-side communication active), with one Assistant-led cell and zero Silent or Human-led cells; the Silent quadrant that occupied \(44\%\) of cells on \texttt{make\_cereal} disappears entirely.
Two structural effects of the longer rollout drive this collapse.
First, with the same two critical steps spread over \(2.5\!\times\) the ticks, the per-episode failure probability under the un-aided memory trajectory is large enough that even the most expensive cost cell clears the communication threshold: the policy cannot afford to stay silent anywhere on the grid.
Second, the longer horizon admits both channels in the same cell --- the assistant's belief tracker accrues enough evidence to time reminders well, while the human still benefits from cheap narrations to keep its own memory of the next critical step active --- so the cost-asymmetry axis no longer cleanly partitions the grid into single-channel quadrants.
The division-of-labor index across active cells averages \(\mathrm{DoL} = 0.46\) on \texttt{latte\_making} (vs.~\(0.79\) on \texttt{make\_cereal}), consistent with a near-even split rather than a sharp role swap.
We read the result as evidence that the framework's response to task length is \emph{itself} regime-dependent: the same cost-asymmetry axis that produces sharp role swaps on a short procedure folds into the Mixed regime on a longer one --- a finding that would have been invisible from the headline-task grid alone, and that motivates the planned 7-task expansion (Section~\ref{sec:val-summary}) as a way to disentangle the criticality-density and length axes that this two-task slice confounds.

\subsection{Summary}
\label{sec:val-summary}

The per-axis sweeps on \texttt{make\_cereal} establish that the learned policy is not a single fixed strategy but a regime-conditional family:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{C1 (communication regime):} cost asymmetry between
        agents produces a sharp role swap in the learned policy, and
        the per-cell improvement panel (Figure~\ref{fig:phase-asymmetric-baselines})
        shows that the regime-conditional choice beats both the
        rule-based Heuristic and the zero-communication None floor
        across the active cells of the cost-asymmetry grid
        (Section~\ref{sec:val-asymmetric}).
  \item \textbf{C2 (memory dynamics):} the framework's response to
        the memory axis --- initial level and decay rate --- is the
        empirical anchor for the next revision
        (Section~\ref{sec:val-memory}).
  \item \textbf{C3 (sensing fidelity):} the joint policy responds to
        the assistant's sensing conditions, deploying narration where
        it is durable and affordable, and falling back on confirmation
        where it is not (Section~\ref{sec:val-sensing}).
\end{itemize}

The two-task replication (Section~\ref{sec:val-multitask}) shows that the four-class cost-asymmetry topology does \emph{not} transfer naively to a procedure of \(2.5\!\times\) the rollout length: the Silent and Human-led phases vanish and the policy collapses into a near-uniform Mixed regime (\(98\%\) of cells), because aggregate failure risk over the longer rollout clears the communication threshold in every cost cell.
This is itself a prediction of the framework --- the expected failure cost is the product of memory-modulated failure probability and per-step failure cost, and a longer rollout multiplies the former --- but it would have been invisible from the single-task grid, and it identifies task length as a dimension that the validation campaign must control rather than average over.
The remaining step of that campaign, deferred to the next revision, is the 7-task expansion: sweeping the same cost-asymmetry grid on \texttt{make\_coffee}, \texttt{make\_sandwich}, \texttt{make\_tea}, \texttt{cooking}, and \texttt{make\_stencil}, and reporting a cross-task comparison that disentangles the criticality-density and task-length axes the present two-task slice confounds.
